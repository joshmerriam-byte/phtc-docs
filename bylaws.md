### About These Bylaws

These Bylaws hold the operational detail the Constitution deliberately leaves out, so the Society can adjust how it works without amending its founding document each time experience teaches it something. Any Bylaw must be consistent with the Constitution, the Society's charitable purposes, the Incorporated Societies Act 2022, and the Charities Act 2005. Where a Bylaw and the Constitution conflict, the Constitution prevails. The Committee may amend these Bylaws by Ordinary Resolution, subject to the reservation in clause 13 of the Constitution that no Bylaw may alter who holds a vote or voice. Changes are recorded with a version note and reported to the next Council of Stewards meeting.

## 1.  Membership Operations

### 1.1  Annual Dues

Dues are set each year by Ordinary Resolution at the AGM. The starting schedule is:

| Category | Annual Dues | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Member Organisation | $150 | Equal for all organisations regardless of size; covers the Organisational Seat |
| Zone Steward | $25 | Same as the base Supporting Rider rate |
| Supporting Rider | $25 | The base membership rate |

Hardship is handled by the fee waiver in 1.3, not a separate concession rate. The Member Organisation rate is roughly six times the individual rate, reflecting greater capacity, while staying low enough not to deter smaller clubs.

### 1.2  Joining and Renewal

Supporting Riders join through the Society's membership platform (currently Hivepass) or website, and membership takes effect on payment. Member Organisations and Zone Stewards apply to the Committee, who confirm admission within 30 days. Membership runs for one year from the date of payment. The Society sends one renewal reminder before expiry. Membership lapses automatically if dues remain unpaid 60 days after the due date.

### 1.3  Steward Dues and Fee Waiver

Every Zone Steward must also hold Supporting Rider membership. Where Supporting Rider dues would be a genuine barrier for a steward doing valuable work, the steward may ask the Committee in writing, or verbally to any officer, for a waiver. The Committee may grant a full or partial waiver. Waivers are recorded in the member register and reviewed annually. No one is priced out of stewardship.

### 1.4  Supporting Rider Recognition

Supporting Rider membership is a way to give back to the trails, not a service bought in exchange for dues. In recognition of their contribution, Supporting Riders receive:

- Quarterly trail condition updates across all zones, a practical resource for planning rides;

- The annual contribution report, showing what their dues helped fund;

- Notification of working bees and dig days;

- Access to the Society's personal locator beacons under the policy in 1.5;

- Participation in the quarterly member survey, with results reported to the Council of Stewards.

Founding Members receive permanent recognition in the annual report and the first year of Supporting Rider membership at no cost.  [CMBC enrolment feeder is CMBC-TIED — see Constitution 4.8, 17.4]

Recognition is framed as information flows and acknowledgement, not exclusive perks, consistent with the donation basis of Supporting Rider membership.

### 1.5  Personal Locator Beacon Policy

The Society holds personal locator beacons (PLBs), inherited from Canterbury Mountain Bike Club, available to current financial members for any ride or outdoor activity. [CMBC-TIED origin] Current holders are recorded in the equipment register. To borrow a PLB, a member contacts a listed holder. Society activities take priority over personal use. Beacons must be returned promptly and any activation reported. The Committee reviews PLB battery and registration currency annually.

### 1.6  Member Conduct

Members agree to promote the purposes of the Society, act in good faith toward the wider network, and do nothing to bring the Society into disrepute. Detailed conduct expectations are in Section 10.

## 2.  Zones and the Steward Register

### 2.1  The Zone Framework

The Port Hills trail network is divided into named zones, based on the framework developed for the Track Heroes. Each zone has defined approximate boundaries, one or more trails or trail sections, and ideally a named steward. A zone may consist of a single trail. Zones use the spine-and-spokes framing: the Summit Road ridgeline as the spine, with valleys and trail clusters as the spokes running off it. The Committee defines and maintains zone boundaries in the zone register, and a zone must be a coherent unit rather than a subdivision created to generate additional seats.

### 2.2  What Active Stewardship Means

A zone steward is someone who, in practice: regularly inspects their zone's trails; carries out or organises routine maintenance; files a condition report before each quarterly meeting; logs volunteer hours; and acts as the contact point for their zone. The quarterly condition report is the minimum record of active stewardship, and a missed report is what triggers the lapse process in clause 4.6 of the Constitution. Organising others counts, and a steward need not do everything alone.

### 2.3  The Steward Register

The Society maintains a zone steward register recording, for each zone: the zone name and approximate boundaries; the current steward's name and contact; the trails and trail sections within it; the current condition rating; the date of the last recorded working session; and the steward's status (active, lapsed, seeking, or paused). Where a steward has notified a temporary pause, the register records the pause and any temporary stand-in recognised by the Committee. The register is maintained by the Coordinator once in post, and by the Secretary or a nominated person until then.

### 2.4  New Stewards and Vacant Zones

A person taking on active stewardship of a zone files an initial condition report for that zone, which serves as their self-nomination, and is then recognised by the Committee and added to the register, and, if they wish a vote, admitted as a Zone Steward. The initial report may be brief: a short current assessment of the zone is enough. It exists to demonstrate real stewardship, not to create a barrier. A zone with no active steward is flagged in the register and is a standing item at Council of Stewards meetings until filled. The Society actively encourages Supporting Riders and others to adopt vacant zones.

## 3.  Trail Condition Rating System

### 3.1  The A–F Scale

Stewards rate each trail or trail section before each quarterly meeting using a consistent A–F scale, so the Society can track network health over time. The scale runs: A+ exceptional (monitoring only); A well maintained (minor seasonal upkeep); B showing wear (one or two working bees per season); C deteriorating (working bee needed soon); D significant problems (planned project response needed); F failing or closed (major intervention required).

### 3.2  Trend Reporting

Alongside the current grade, the steward notes the trend since the last quarter: improving, stable, or declining. The trend matters as much as the grade. A C trending up tells a very different story from a C trending down.

### 3.3  How Ratings Are Used

Ratings feed three things: the quarterly condition update to Supporting Riders; the project scoring system, where need is scored from the condition rating; and the annual contribution report, which shows each zone's rating at the start and end of the year so improvement is visible. Ratings also serve a liability function: a documented rating and a flagged feature show the Society is monitoring conditions diligently.

## 4.  Volunteer Hours and Reporting

### 4.1  Logging Sessions

All working sessions on Port Hills trails, whether maintenance, new build, or planting, are logged using the Society's shared reporting form. The form captures the date, zone, number of participants, hours worked, and a brief description. It is linked from the Society website for easy mobile access. Stewards are encouraged to log sessions on the day rather than from memory.

### 4.2  What Counts

Countable hours include trail maintenance and construction, drainage and erosion work, planting and pest control alongside trail corridors, condition inspections, and the organising and supervision time directly supporting a working session. Travel time is not counted.

### 4.3  Compiling and Using Hours

The Coordinator, or until then a nominated person, compiles a quarterly summary from the form data for each Council of Stewards meeting. The annual total is a central figure in grant applications and in the annual contribution report. Volunteer hours, valued at an accepted hourly rate, also serve as in-kind contribution to match funding in grant applications.

## 5.  Meeting Procedures

### 5.1  Standard Council of Stewards Agenda

Each quarterly Council of Stewards meeting follows a standard agenda: welcome, apologies, and confirmation of the previous summary; trail condition updates from each zone steward, with current rating and trend; volunteer hours summary for the quarter; results of the quarterly member survey; Coordinator's report once in post; Committee's report, including any decisions taken since the last meeting and any departures from Council direction with reasons; project nominations, scoring review, and priority discussion; grant applications in progress and results received; working bee coordination and shared calendar review; and any other business.

### 5.2  Notice and Minutes

Voting members receive at least 14 days notice of each quarterly meeting and 21 days notice of the AGM. A summary of each meeting, covering key points, recommendations, decisions, and actions, is circulated to all voting members within 14 days via the Google Group. Where a steward cannot attend, they may send their condition update and hours in writing for tabling.

### 5.3  Electronic Participation

Meetings may be held in person, by audio-visual link, or a combination. Members joining remotely have the same speaking and voting rights as those present, provided all participants can communicate effectively and simultaneously.

## 6.  Project Assessment and Funding Allocation

### 6.1  Two Streams

The Society distinguishes routine maintenance, being ongoing upkeep within a zone, from capital projects, being new build, major reroutes, and significant improvements. Each has its own allocation approach.

### 6.2  Maintenance Allocation Factors

Where Society funds are available for routine maintenance, they are allocated across zones guided by the following factors, as a starting point for discussion: trail length under active stewardship (25%, kilometres of maintained trail); rider and user volumes (25%, from counts, Trailforks data, and steward knowledge); historical maintenance record (20%, volunteer hours logged and consistency of upkeep); current condition (20%, A–F rating, lower grades indicating greater need); and zone equity (10%, adjustment per the equity mechanism in 6.5).

### 6.3  Capital Project Scoring

Capital projects requiring Society resources or advocacy are scored by the Coordinator against the following criteria, presented to the Council of Stewards for a ranked-choice vote, as a starting point: need (30%, from condition rating, F=5 down to A=1); readiness (30%, landowner consent in place, plan developed, steward committed to lead); network value (20%, riders benefited, connections improved, spine or spoke significance); and zone equity (20%, adjustment per the equity mechanism in 6.5).

### 6.4  Ranked-Choice Vote and Committee Role

The Coordinator presents scored assessments to the Council of Stewards, which votes on project priorities using ranked-choice voting. The Committee gives effect to the Council's priorities. Where the Committee cannot or should not implement a priority, because consent falls through, funding shifts, or a landowner withdraws, it records its reasons in writing and reports them to the next Council of Stewards meeting, consistent with the Constitution.

### 6.5  Zone Equity Mechanism

This is the operational mechanism implementing the zone equity principle in clause 11 of the Constitution. The Society maintains a simple record of which zones have received Society-funded support in each annual cycle. Before each allocation round, the Coordinator reviews this record and flags any zone passed over for two or more consecutive cycles despite having stewardship and need.

A flagged zone receives an equity adjustment in its score for that round. The adjustment is not automatic funding: it is a thumb on the scale to surface zones the criteria would otherwise consistently overlook. The Coordinator presents flagged zones openly to the Council of Stewards, which consciously decides whether special consideration is warranted. Active zones with strong, ready projects are not penalised; the mechanism only ensures structurally disadvantaged zones are seen and considered.

Worked example: a small zone with one steward, low rider counts, and no shovel-ready capital project would lose every capital round on the raw criteria. If it has been passed over for two cycles, the equity flag surfaces it, the Council sees the pattern, and may direct a modest maintenance allocation or a coordinator visit even though it did not win the capital vote. A large, active zone winning rounds on merit keeps doing so.

### 6.6  Emergency Repairs

Where a trail suffers sudden significant damage from extreme weather, slips, floods, or similar events outside the steward's control, the Committee may allocate emergency repair funds without the standard assessment process. Emergency allocations require the approval of at least two Committee officers and are reported to the Council of Stewards at the next meeting. Emergency repairs do not count against a zone's allocation history for equity purposes. The Committee aims to hold a modest emergency reserve and to identify rapid-response funding pathways as part of annual financial planning.

## 7.  The Coordinator

### 7.1  Purpose of the Role

The Coordinator exists to carry the administrative load that currently falls on volunteers, so zone stewards can focus on the trails. The role is equivalent to a part-time general manager, operating under the direction of the Committee and reporting to the Council of Stewards quarterly.

### 7.2  Responsibilities

The Coordinator is responsible for: maintaining the shared trail calendar and network communications; managing the volunteer hours reporting system and producing quarterly condition reports; writing and coordinating grant applications; liaising with CCC, DOC, the Summit Road Society, and other landowners; maintaining the zone steward register and the Society's records; administering the Supporting Rider programme, including surveys and PLB bookings; preparing meeting agendas and circulating minutes; keeping the Society's website, calendar, and public presence current; scoring capital projects and presenting assessments to the Council of Stewards; and securing funding to sustain the role beyond its first year.

### 7.3  Authority and Reporting

The Coordinator acts within authority delegated by the Committee and a written position description. They attend Committee and Council of Stewards meetings without a vote. Spending authority and any delegations are set by the Committee and recorded. The Coordinator's performance is reviewed annually by the Committee against the position description and agreed objectives.

### 7.4  Funding the Role

The role is funded primarily through grants and external income rather than member dues. The Coordinator's first-year priority is to secure funding to sustain the role into its second year. Engaging a Coordinator is a goal of the Society, not a precondition for operating.

## 8.  Financial Procedures

### 8.1  Expense Reimbursement

Members and Officers may claim reimbursement of actual and reasonable expenses incurred on the Society's behalf, supported by a receipt and approved by an officer who did not incur the expense. Reimbursement is not a distribution of profit and does not breach the no-personal-benefit rule.

### 8.2  Donor and Sponsor Recognition

The Society recognises financial supporters who are not members through two non-voting categories. Supporting Partners are businesses and organisations sponsoring the Society, typically at or above a Member Organisation's annual dues, named in the annual report, sometimes invited to attend and speak at Council of Stewards meetings, and acknowledged on the website. Patrons are individuals lending their name and support, named in the annual report and invited to attend and speak at meetings.

No level of contribution converts into a governance vote. Governance seats are earned only through active stewardship or the Council-confirmed Public Representative role. This protects both the federated principle and the Society's charitable standing.

### 8.3  Donations

The Society may receive donations through any lawful channel, kept separate from membership and accounted for accordingly. Larger or variable donations, including tool-linked giving or a coordinator-funding campaign, are handled operationally and recorded, but do not confer membership or any governance seat.

### 8.4  Grant Applications

Grant applications are coordinated by the Coordinator, or the Committee until then, and approved by the Committee before submission. The Society keeps a register of applications, outcomes, and any conditions attached, so grant conditions are tracked and honoured. Volunteer hours are used as in-kind match where a funder allows.

### 8.5  Disclosure of Other Governance Roles

Every voting member discloses to the Committee any elected or appointed governance role they hold in another organisation doing similar work or pursuing similar aims, such as trails, cycling, or land management in the region, including committee membership, office, or trusteeship. Disclosures are recorded in the interests register and updated as roles change. This gives the Council visibility of any concentration of influence without limiting who may hold a seat earned through stewardship.

### 8.6  Annual Contribution Report

The Committee publishes a public annual contribution report after the end of each financial year, covering: total volunteer hours by zone and quarter; trail condition ratings and trends; projects completed; grants received and how funds were spent; Coordinator activities; zone equity status; and contractor work by zone. The report is shared with all members, CCC, DOC, ECan where relevant, and partner bodies. It is the Society's primary accountability document and a key credibility artifact for funders.

## 9.  Communications and Public Presence

### 9.1  Channels

The Society maintains a website (phtc.org.nz), a shared public calendar of working bees and events, a Google Group for member and steward coordination, and social media as the Committee decides. The Coordinator keeps these current once in post; until then a nominated person does.

### 9.2  Who Speaks for the Society

The Chair is the Society's primary external voice with CCC, DOC, and partner bodies. Other members may speak about their own zones and work, but commitments on behalf of the Society are made only by the Committee or its delegate. Public statements on contested matters are agreed by the Committee. The Public Representative is the Society's public point of contact for riders and the wider public.

### 9.3  Trail Signage

The Society provides a standard trail sign template for zones, identifying the trail, the steward or club that maintains it, and the Society, with a QR code linking to the website where people can get involved, become a Supporting Rider, donate, or log hours. Signs use a consistent format and durable weatherproof material. Placement respects landowner permissions.

## 10.  Codes of Conduct

### 10.1  Member Conduct

Members are expected to treat other trail users, volunteers, landowners, and rangers with respect; follow the New Zealand Mountain Bike Code and share trails courteously; represent the Society positively; and raise concerns through the proper channels rather than public disputes.

### 10.2  Landowner Respect and Authorised Trails

The Society and its members do not build, ride, or endorse riding on trails where this is prohibited by the landowner or land manager. Building on land without permission is not Society activity and is not supported. The Society's standing with CCC, DOC, and private landowners depends on this being upheld without exception. Nothing here requires a member to account for or renounce trail use or work undertaken in their own private capacity outside Society activity, consistent with clause 3.5 of the Constitution.

### 10.3  Health and Safety at Working Bees

Working bees follow a basic health and safety standard: a briefing at the start covering hazards and first aid; appropriate tools and protective equipment; a named person responsible for safety on the day; a sign-in recording who is present; and an incident report process. The Committee maintains and reviews a working bee safety procedure.

## 11.  Dispute Resolution

The dispute resolution procedure is set out in clause 12 of the Constitution, which adopts clauses 2 to 8 of Schedule 2 of the Act. This section adds only operational detail: complaints are sent in writing to the Secretary; the Committee acknowledges receipt within 10 Working Days; and the Committee favours the least adversarial resolution that fairly addresses the matter. Nothing in this section overrides clause 12 of the Constitution.
